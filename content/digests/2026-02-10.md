### [Coalition Urges California to Revoke Permits for Federal License Plate Reader Surveillance ](https://www.eff.org/press/releases/coalition-urges-california-revoke-permits-federal-license-plate-reader-surveillance)
*EFF*

A coalition of organizations, led by EFF and Imperial Valley Equity & Justice, has urged California Governor Gavin Newsom to revoke permits allowing federal agencies like Customs and Border Patrol (CBP) and the Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA) to install automated license plate readers (ALPRs) along border highways. These surveillance devices put residents and migrants at risk of harassment, abuse, detention, and deportation.

The coalition's letter to Governor Newsom and Caltrans Director Dina El-Tawansy notes that this invasive mass surveillance is particularly urgent given the return of U.S. Border Patrol Chief Greg Bovino to the El Centro sector, which has been associated with a brutal campaign against immigrants and U.S. citizens. The coalition includes organizations like the California Nurses Association, ACLU California Action, and Project on Government Oversight.

In essence, this article reveals that federal agencies are using license plate readers to surveil residents and migrants in California, putting them at risk of serious harm. This is happening despite concerns about the invasive nature of such surveillance and its potential for abuse. The coalition's demand for revocation of permits highlights the need for greater oversight and accountability in the use of these devices.

The article emphasizes the urgent need for action to prevent further harm, citing the return of a notorious border patrol chief as a key factor. This suggests that the coalition is framing this issue as a matter of immediate concern, rather than a long-term problem that can be addressed later.

Overall, this article provides a clear example of how federal agencies are using surveillance technologies to exert control over marginalized communities in California. The coalition's efforts to revoke permits and prevent further harm highlight the need for greater transparency and accountability in government use of such technologies.

### [EFFecting Change: Get the Flock Out of Our City](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2026/02/effecting-change-get-flock-out-our-city)
*EFF*

The Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF) is hosting an event to discuss growing resistance against Flock, a company that provides Automated License Plate Readers (ALPRs) for surveillance purposes. Cities across the US are rejecting Flock contracts due to concerns over civil liberties and privacy.

Flock's ALPR technology has enabled surveillance of people seeking abortions, protesters exercising First Amendment rights, and communities targeted by discriminatory policing. This is why cities like Austin, Cambridge, and small towns in Texas have started saying no to Flock contracts, proving that surveillance isn't inevitable – it's a choice.

EFF reports that the event will feature a panel discussion with experts from EFF, Fight for the Future, and Rural Privacy Coalition, exploring the implications of Flock's technology and successful campaigns that have stopped deployments. The livestream is free and open to the public, and will be live-captioned and recorded.

As EFF details, this resistance against Flock marks a significant shift in how cities approach surveillance technology, with many now recognizing that it erodes civil liberties from the moment it's installed.

### [The Internet Still Works: Yelp Protects Consumer Reviews](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2026/02/internet-still-works-yelp-protects-consumer-reviews)
*EFF*

Yelp has fought major legal battles to defend reviewers' rights and preserve protections that allow consumers to share honest feedback online, according to EFF's interview with Aaron Schur, General Counsel at Yelp.

This matters because Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act shields Yelp from liability for user-generated content, allowing it to host millions of reviews without fear of lawsuits. As Jacob Rogers, Associate General Counsel at the Wikimedia Foundation, notes: "Section 230 helps make it possible for online communities to host user speech: from restaurant reviews, to fan fiction, to collaborative encyclopedias."

The article emphasizes that without Section 230's protection, Wikipedia would not be able to maintain its community-driven approach to content creation and moderation.

### [Databricks CEO says SaaS isn’t dead, but AI will soon make it irrelevant](https://techcrunch.com/2026/02/09/databricks-ceo-says-saas-isnt-dead-but-ai-will-soon-make-it-irrelevant/)
*TechCrunch*

On Monday, Databricks announced it reached a $5.4 billion revenue run rate, growing 65% year-over-year, of which more than $1.4 billion was from its AI products.

Co-founder and CEO Ali Ghodsi wanted to share these growth numbers because there’s so much talk about how AI is going to kill the SaaS business, he told TechCrunch.

“Everybody’s like, ‘Oh, it’s SaaS. What’s going to happen to all these companies? What’s AI going to do with all these companies?’ For us, it’s just increasing the usage,” he said.

To be sure, he also wants to distance Databricks from the SaaS label, given that private markets value it as an AI company. Databricks on Monday also officially closed on its massive, previously announced $5 billion raise at a $134 billion valuation, and nabbed a $2 billion loan facility as well.

But the company is straddling both worlds. Databricks is still best known as a cloud data warehouse provider. A data warehouse is where enterprises store massive amounts of data to analyze for business insights.

Ghodsi called out, in particular, one AI product that’s driving usage of its data warehouse: its LLM user interface named Genie.

Genie is an example of how a SaaS business can replace its user interface with natural language. For instance, he uses it to ask why warehouse usage and revenue spike on particular days.

Just a few years ago, such a request required writing queries in a specific technical language, or having a special report programmed. Today, any product with an LLM interface can be used by anyone, Ghodsi noted. Genie is one reason for the company’s usage growth numbers, he said.

The threat of AI to SaaS isn’t, as one AI VC jokingly tweeted, that it will replace all SaaS companies. Rather, it will change how they operate and interact with their customers.

### [ChatGPT rolls out ads](https://techcrunch.com/2026/02/09/chatgpt-rolls-out-ads/)
*TechCrunch*

OpenAI on Monday announced it’s beginning to test ads in the U.S. for users on its Free and Go subscription tiers.

The newer Go plan is a low-cost subscription at $8 per month in the U.S. and was introduced globally in mid-January.

Subscribers to OpenAI’s paid plans, including its Plus, Pro, Business, Enterprise, and Education tiers, will not see ads, the company said.

OpenAI sought to address concerns about how ads might affect the user experience, stating in a blog post: “Ads do not influence the answers ChatGPT gives you, and we keep your conversations with ChatGPT private from advertisers. Our goal is for ads to support broader access to more powerful ChatGPT features while maintaining the trust people place in ChatGPT for important and personal tasks.”

The move, which the company announced last month, drew ridicule in a series of Super Bowl ads that ran on Sunday from a top rival, Anthropic.

In its TV commercials, Anthropic poked fun at the idea that some AI companies, like OpenAI, would soon include advertising by showing how poorly integrated ads could disrupt the consumer experience. This was portrayed on-screen by glassy-eyed actors playing AI chatbots, who would deliver their advice alongside a poorly targeted ad.

OpenAI CEO Sam Altman got extremely testy about the jabs, calling them “dishonest” and Anthropic an “authoritarian company.”
