### [Homeland Security is trying to force tech companies to hand over data about Trump critics](https://techcrunch.com/2026/02/03/homeland-security-is-trying-to-force-tech-companies-to-hand-over-data-about-trump-critics/)
This article reveals a concerning trend where the Department of Homeland Security (DHS) is using administrative subpoenas to demand user information from tech companies about individuals who criticize the Trump administration. According to techcrunch, DHS has been targeting anonymous Instagram accounts that share posts about ICE immigration raids and those who have criticized Trump officials or protested government policies.

What's striking is how these subpoenas bypass judicial oversight, allowing investigators to seek a wealth of information without a judge's approval. This raises serious concerns about the concentration of power and the potential for abuse by the government. As the article notes, administrative subpoenas can demand identifiable information such as login times, devices used, and email addresses, which could be used to intimidate or silence critics.

The story matters because it highlights how the government is using technology to monitor and suppress dissenting voices. The fact that DHS has withdrawn its subpoena in at least four cases after account owners sued suggests a deliberate attempt to intimidate people who document immigration activity or criticize government actions. This is a clear example of how power dynamics are shifting, with the government increasingly relying on administrative measures to exert control over citizens.

The article also underscores the importance of data sovereignty and the need for tech companies to protect user information. While tech companies may not be required to comply with these subpoenas, they often do so voluntarily, which raises questions about their role in facilitating government surveillance. Ultimately, this story highlights the tension between national security concerns and individual rights, and how the use of administrative subpoenas can erode trust in institutions and undermine democratic values.
