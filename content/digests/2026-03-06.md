## Deep Dives

### [Italian prosecutors confirm journalist was hacked with Paragon spyware](https://techcrunch.com/2026/03/05/italian-prosecutors-confirm-journalist-was-hacked-with-paragon-spyware/)
*TechCrunch*

The Italian prosecutors have confirmed that journalist Francesco Cancellato was indeed hacked with Paragon spyware in December 2024. This development comes after a technical report found evidence of the hack on Cancellato's phone, as well as those of two other individuals, Giuseppe Caccia and Luca Casarini.

What's striking is that this confirmation contradicts previous official investigations that failed to confirm the hack. The Italian government had previously denied involvement in the hacking scandal, but now it seems that there may have been a cover-up. TechCrunch reports that the prosecutors' offices will continue to investigate to identify Cancellato's hackers.

This case highlights concerns about the use of commercial surveillance vendors by governments and intelligence agencies. Paragon spyware has been linked to several hacking scandals in the past, and its sale to American private equity firm AE Industrial has raised eyebrows. The fact that Italian authorities inspected the Paragon spyware server used by the intelligence agency AISI to target the phones of its targets suggests a disturbing level of complicity between governments and surveillance vendors.

The hack also raises questions about the safety and security of journalists and activists who use digital communication tools. WhatsApp alerted Cancellato and around 90 other people, including journalists and members of civil society, that they had been targeted with spyware made by Paragon Solutions in January 2025. This incident highlights the need for greater transparency and accountability from governments and surveillance vendors.

As TechCrunch notes, the full report is not yet public, but it's clear that this case has significant implications for data sovereignty and the use of surveillance technology by governments.

### [Weasel Words: OpenAI’s Pentagon Deal Won’t Stop AI‑Powered Surveillance](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2026/03/weasel-words-openais-pentagon-deal-wont-stop-ai-powered-surveillance)
*EFF*

OpenAI's deal with the Pentagon has sparked widespread criticism for its potential to enable mass surveillance. Despite CEO Sam Altman's concession that the initial agreement was “opportunistic and sloppy,” the company has amended the contract to include language that may not prevent domestic surveillance.

Critics argue that OpenAI's attempts to ensure compliance through secret agreements and technical assurances are insufficient, as they do not provide strong, enforceable legal limits or transparency. EFF notes that the U.S. government has historically used a loose interpretation of “applicable laws” to justify mass surveillance, citing examples such as the Fourth Amendment and FISA Act.

### [Dean Ball on open models and government control](https://www.interconnects.ai/p/how-anthropic-vs-dow-impacts-open)
*Interconnects*

The article highlights a significant development in the AI landscape, where the Department of War (DoW) has designated Anthropic as a supply chain risk. This move has sparked a discussion about the implications for open models in AI and their potential to become the stable equilibrium for power centers.

According to Dean Ball, this designation could be a turning point in perspectives on open models, with existing power structures grappling with their roles relative to existing companies. He argues that open models may avoid some of the power struggles seen recently due to their decentralized nature.

### [Waiting for the check marks: The reality of connecting with family in Iran](https://restofworld.org/2026/iran-internet-shutdown-diaspora-family/?utm_source=rss&utm_medium=rss&utm_campaign=feeds)
*Rest of World*

Iranians are forced to rely on digital breadcrumbs, like two check marks on a messaging app, to confirm their loved ones have received and read their messages. This ritual highlights the emotional toll of living under internet restrictions and surveillance.

The article reveals a stark contrast between technology's potential for connection and its limitations imposed by state control. In Iran, even small moments of communication can be fraught with anxiety, as individuals wait for confirmation that their messages have been received.

Per Rest of World, the Iranian government's restrictions on internet access have created a culture of uncertainty around digital communication. This has led to a reliance on subtle cues, like check marks, to gauge the success of online interactions.
