## The Big Picture
The designation of Anthropic as a "supply chain risk" by the Department of Defense has ignited a heated debate about the boundaries between government, corporate, and activist spheres. Meanwhile, hacktivists have claimed responsibility for hacking Homeland Security to release allegedly stolen ICE contract data, highlighting the tension between state control and decentralized disruption. As the lines between these groups blur, traditional notions of accountability are being threatened. The simultaneous rise of AI-powered tools like 14.ai, which is replacing customer support teams at startups, and the proliferation of prediction markets on platforms like Polymarket, where users can bet on the timing of a US-Israeli military strike against Iran, underscore the growing importance of digital infrastructure in modern conflict.

### [Hacktivists claim to have hacked Homeland Security to release ICE contract data](https://techcrunch.com/2026/03/02/hacktivists-claim-to-have-hacked-homeland-security-to-release-ice-contract-data/)
*TechCrunch*

A group of hacktivists, calling themselves "Department of Peace," has claimed responsibility for hacking the Department of Homeland Security (DHS) and releasing allegedly stolen documents online. The leaked data includes contracts between DHS, Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE), and over 6,000 companies, including tech giants Microsoft and Oracle.

The hacktivists' motive is revealed in a document accompanying the leak: they cited the recent killings of two peaceful protesters by federal agents as their reason for releasing the data. The leaked information includes contractor names, contract amounts, contact information, and other details, making it easily searchable on a dedicated website organized by security researcher Micah Lee.

This development highlights the involvement of various companies in ICE's deportation efforts, which critics argue are carried out in inhumane conditions. TechCrunch reports that Palantir has been at the forefront of providing technology to aid these deportations. The leaked data also shows that some contractors have received significant funding for their work with ICE, including $70 million for Cyber Apex Solutions and $59 million for Science Applications International Corporation.

The hacktivists' actions may be seen as a form of resistance against oppressive systems, but the article does not explicitly advocate for non-cooperation. The leaked data, however, provides valuable information about the companies involved in supporting ICE's deportation efforts, which could inform future activism and policy changes.

### [Hackers and internet outages hit Iran amid US air strikes](https://techcrunch.com/2026/03/02/hackers-and-internet-outages-hit-iran-amid-u-s-air-strikes/)
*TechCrunch*

The article reveals that a coordinated cyberattack and airstrikes have crippled Iran's infrastructure, with hackers flooding a popular prayer app with anti-government messages and causing widespread internet outages.

As reported by TechCrunch, the US and Israeli coalition has launched airstrikes against Iranian targets, killing the country's supreme leader Ali Hosseini Khamenei and top leadership. The attacks coincided with cyberattacks aimed at limiting Iran's response, including a hack of the BadeSaba prayer app that sent notifications calling for a "reckoning" and promising amnesty to those who rise up against government forces.

The article highlights the use of cyber warfare as a tool of modern conflict, with both sides employing digital attacks to gain an advantage. The hack of the prayer app is particularly noteworthy, as it suggests that even seemingly innocuous platforms can be co-opted for malicious purposes.

Meanwhile, the Iranian internet has collapsed, with connectivity dropping to near-zero levels. This has significant implications for e-commerce and shipping routes in the Middle East, which rely on online infrastructure to function.

The article also reveals a power struggle between Iran's government and its citizens, with the regime facing growing resistance from within. The hack of the prayer app is seen by some as an attempt to further destabilize the situation, while others view it as a desperate bid for change.

As TechCrunch notes, this conflict has far-reaching implications for global security and the role of technology in modern warfare.

### [AI Bros Wanted Trump. Now They Learn What Happens When You Tell Him No.](https://www.techdirt.com/2026/03/02/ai-bros-wanted-trump-now-they-learn-what-happens-when-you-tell-him-no/)
*Tech Dirt*

The US government has declared AI company Anthropic a "supply chain risk" due to its refusal to work with the military on certain projects, specifically those involving domestic mass surveillance or autonomous killing machines without human oversight.

This move is significant because it highlights the risks of working with a government that can turn on businesses at any moment. The Trump administration's attempt to destroy Anthropic for pushing back on its policies demonstrates the dangers of cozying up to authoritarians. Tech Dirt reports that this incident echoes warnings made last year about the dangers of innovation in an environment where the rules change based on political caprice.

As Tech Dirt notes, "Innovation requires trust... None of that works in an environment where the rules change based on political caprice." The article emphasizes that Anthropic's red line was modest - they simply said their AI shouldn't make kill decisions without a human in the loop and shouldn't be used for mass surveillance. This move is seen as an attempt by the Trump administration to destroy a tech company that pushed back on its policies, rather than addressing any actual supply chain risk.

The article also highlights the double standard of "supply chain risk" designations, which are often used to prop up American companies against foreign competitors with little evidence regarding the actual risk. In this case, there is no underlying risk - Anthropic's refusal to work on certain projects is seen as a betrayal by the Trump administration.

The incident has significant implications for the AI industry, which had been enthusiastic about working with the Trump administration. As Tech Dirt notes, "it makes no sense at all" that Anthropic was declared a supply chain risk due to its refusal to work on certain projects. The article emphasizes that this move is a warning sign for tech companies and entrepreneurs who may be tempted to cozy up to authoritarian governments.

### [Tech workers urge DOD, Congress to withdraw Anthropic label as a supply chain risk](https://techcrunch.com/2026/03/02/tech-workers-urge-dod-congress-to-withdraw-anthropic-label-as-a-supply-chain-risk/)
*TechCrunch*

The Department of Defense (DOD) has designated Anthropic, an AI lab, as a "supply chain risk," which would effectively blacklist it from working with any agency or company that does business with the Pentagon. Hundreds of tech workers from major companies have signed an open letter urging the DOD and Congress to withdraw this label, citing concerns over government overreach in the AI industry.

The dispute centers on Anthropic's refusal to give the military unrestricted access to its AI systems, which it fears could be used for mass surveillance or autonomous weapons. The tech workers argue that this treatment sets a "dangerous precedent" and could lead to coercion by the government. President Trump has directed federal agencies to stop using Anthropic's technology after a six-month transition period, while Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth has threatened to designate Anthropic as a supply chain risk if it doesn't comply.

The tech workers' open letter calls on Congress to examine whether the use of these extraordinary authorities against an American technology company is appropriate. The letter includes signatories from major technology and venture capital firms, including OpenAI, Slack, IBM, and Salesforce Ventures.
