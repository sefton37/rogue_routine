### [Americans are destroying Flock surveillance cameras](https://techcrunch.com/2026/02/23/americans-are-destroying-flock-surveillance-cameras/)
*TechCrunch*

Across the United States, people are taking matters into their own hands by vandalizing and destroying Flock surveillance cameras in protest against the company's license plate readers aiding U.S. immigration authorities and deportations.

This development is significant because it marks a growing backlash against Flock's technology, which has been criticized for allowing federal authorities access to its massive network of nationwide license plate readers and databases. TechCrunch reports that dozens of cities have rejected the use of Flock's cameras, and some police departments have blocked federal authorities from using their resources.

The vandalism is not limited to a single location; cases have been reported in California, Connecticut, Illinois, Oregon, and Virginia. In one instance, six license plate-scanning cameras on poles were cut down and at least one was spray-painted with a message that read: "Hahaha get wrecked ya surveilling fucks." This level of public resistance highlights the growing concern over Flock's role in immigration enforcement.

Flock claims it doesn't share data directly with ICE, but reports show local police have shared access to the cameras and databases with federal authorities. The company has faced criticism for its involvement in the Trump administration's immigration crackdown, which relies heavily on data from Flock's nationwide network of cameras.

The article also highlights the democratization of tools and access, as people are taking matters into their own hands by destroying the cameras. This shift in power dynamics is significant, as it shows that individuals are willing to take action against a company they perceive as complicit in immigration enforcement.

This thread connects to broader patterns of resistance against surveillance technology, particularly in the context of immigration enforcement. The article suggests that Flock's business model, which relies on collecting and sharing data with federal authorities, is increasingly at odds with public sentiment. As more cities cancel contracts with Flock and police departments block federal access to their resources, it becomes clear that the company's role in immigration enforcement is becoming increasingly contentious.
