The article emphasizes the importance of preserving accurate and uncomfortable histories, rather than sanitizing or erasing them. This is a crucial theme in understanding the attention economy and how it shapes our perceptions of reality. By highlighting the Trump administration's efforts to erase historical truths, this story reveals the ways in which power can be used to manipulate public opinion and control the narrative.

The article quotes the judge as saying: > "As if the Ministry of Truth in George Orwell’s 1984 now existed, with its motto ‘Ignorance is Strength,’ this Court is now asked to determine whether the federal government has the power it claims — to dissemble and disassemble historical truths when it has some domain over historical facts. This quote captures the essence of the story: a powerful government attempting to control the narrative and erase uncomfortable truths about America's past. The article's emphasis on this theme is a reminder that even in the face of powerful interests, there are still those who will push back against efforts to distort or manipulate history.

### [Open Letter To Tech Companies: Protect Your Users From Lawless DHS Subpoenas](https://www.techdirt.com/2026/02/20/open-letter-to-tech-companies-protect-your-users-from-lawless-dhs-subpoenas/)
*Tech Dirt*

This article reveals a disturbing power dynamic between tech companies and the Department of Homeland Security (DHS). According to Tech Dirt, the Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF) has sent an open letter to major tech companies like Meta and Google, urging them to protect their users from lawless administrative subpoenas issued by DHS. These subpoenas are often used to target individuals engaged in First Amendment activity, such as documenting ICE activities or attending protests.

The EFF argues that these subpoenas are unlawful and unconstitutional, and that tech companies have a responsibility to resist coercive government demands. They're asking tech companies to insist on court intervention before complying with subpoenas, give users notice when they're targeted, and resist gag orders that prevent them from notifying their users.

This story matters because it highlights the tension between corporate interests and user rights in the digital age. Tech companies are being asked to choose between complying with government demands and protecting their users' freedom of speech and data sovereignty. The EFF's efforts to hold tech companies accountable for their role in this power dynamic are crucial, as they aim to prevent the erosion of civil liberties in the name of national security.

The article also reveals a concerning trend of DHS using administrative subpoenas to silence critics and target protected speech. This is a classic example of how governments can use coercive measures to control public discourse and suppress dissenting voices. The EFF's advocacy efforts are a necessary counterbalance to this overreach, as they push for greater transparency and accountability in the tech industry.

Ultimately, this story highlights the importance of holding power accountable in the digital age. By analyzing the relationships between governments, corporations, and users, we can better understand how these dynamics shape our online experiences and erode or protect our rights.

### [Google VP warns that two types of AI startups may not survive](https://techcrunch.com/2026/02/21/google-vp-warns-that-two-types-of-ai-startups-may-not-survive/)
*TechCrunch*

**Daily Briefing:**

TechCrunch reports that Google's Vice President for Startups, Darren Mowry, is warning that two types of AI startups are unlikely to survive in the long term. According to Mowry, LLM wrappers and AI aggregators are business models that rely too heavily on existing large language models (LLMs) without adding significant value or differentiation.

Mowry specifically calls out LLM wrappers as companies that "wrap very thin intellectual property around Gemini or GPT-5," essentially rebranding someone else's technology. He argues that this approach is unsustainable, as model providers like Google are expanding their enterprise features and squeezing out these types of startups. Mowry also criticizes AI aggregators for struggling to add value beyond mere access to different LLMs.

This warning from a high-ranking executive at Google highlights the challenges faced by many AI startups in the current market. It reveals that the attention economy is driving companies to prioritize short-term gains over long-term sustainability, and that true innovation requires more than just rebranding existing technology or aggregating multiple models.

### [OpenAI debated calling police about suspected Canadian shooter’s chats](https://techcrunch.com/2026/02/21/openai-debated-calling-police-about-suspected-canadian-shooters-chats/)
*TechCrunch*

This article from TechCrunch reveals a disturbing tale of how OpenAI's AI model, ChatGPT, was used by the alleged Tumbler Ridge shooter to describe gun violence and potentially influence others. According to TechCrunch, OpenAI employees flagged these chats in June 2025 due to concerns about gun violence, but ultimately did not report the individual to Canadian authorities.

What makes this story noteworthy is that it highlights a critical blind spot in the attention economy: how online activities can be linked to real-life harm. The article reveals that OpenAI's systems failed to prevent potential harm from their user, raising questions about the responsibility of AI companies to detect and prevent such incidents. As TechCrunch reports, "Our thoughts are with everyone affected by the Tumbler Ridge tragedy," an OpenAI spokesperson said in a statement. "We proactively reached out to the Royal Canadian Mounted Police with information on the individual and their use of ChatGPT, and we'll continue to support their investigation."

This incident is a stark reminder that the attention economy's focus on user engagement and growth can come at the cost of safety and accountability. By not reporting the suspicious activity, OpenAI may have inadvertently enabled harm to occur. The article shines a light on the need for greater transparency and accountability in AI development and deployment, particularly when it comes to detecting and preventing potential harm from users.
