### [ChatGPT uninstalls surged by 295% after DoD deal](https://techcrunch.com/2026/03/02/chatgpt-uninstalls-surged-by-295-after-dod-deal/)
*TechCrunch*

ChatGPT's mobile app saw a significant surge in uninstalls, jumping 295% day-over-day on Saturday, February 28, following news of OpenAI's partnership with the Department of Defense (DoD). This is a substantial increase compared to the app's typical uninstall rate of 9% over the past 30 days. 

The deal has also impacted ChatGPT's download growth, which dropped by 13% day-over-day on Saturday and continued to fall on Sunday, when downloads were down 5% from the previous day. In contrast, OpenAI competitor Anthropic's Claude saw a significant increase in U.S. downloads, rising 37% and 51% day-over-day after announcing it would not partner with the DoD due to concerns over AI surveillance and autonomous weaponry.

Consumers are sharing their opinions about OpenAI's deal through app ratings, with 1-star reviews for ChatGPT surging and five-star reviews declining. Claude has also seen a surge in popularity, hitting No. 1 on the U.S. App Store on Saturday and continuing to sit at that rank as of Monday.

TechCrunch reports that Anthropic's decision not to partner with the DoD was likely influenced by concerns over AI surveillance and autonomous weaponry. This move appears to have resonated with consumers, who are now favoring Claude over ChatGPT. The data suggests that OpenAI's partnership with the DoD has had a significant impact on consumer trust in the company.

Per TechCrunch, market intelligence provider Sensor Tower found that U.S. app uninstalls of ChatGPT jumped 295% day-over-day on Saturday, while downloads for Claude increased by 37% and 51% day-over-day after Anthropic announced its decision not to partner with the DoD. Appfigures also noted that Claude's total daily U.S. downloads on Saturday surpassed those of ChatGPT for the first time.

### [A new app alerts you if someone nearby is wearing smart glasses](https://techcrunch.com/2026/03/02/nearby-glasses-new-app-alerts-you-wearing-smart-glasses-surveillance-meta-snap-bluetooth/)
*TechCrunch*

The article reports on a new Android app called Nearby Glasses that detects and alerts users when someone nearby is wearing smart glasses or other always-recording devices. The app scans for Bluetooth signals from wearable devices made by companies like Meta, Oakley, and Snap, sending a push notification to the user if it detects a potential device in the area.

The app's creator, Yves Jeanrenaud, was inspired by reporting on wearable surveillance devices and aims to help users protect themselves from unwanted recording. TechCrunch reports that Jeanrenaud was particularly concerned about Meta's decision to implement facial recognition as a default feature in its smart glasses, which he considers "a huge floodgate pushed open for all kinds of privacy-invasive behavior."

The app works by listening for nearby Bluetooth signals that contain a publicly assigned identifier unique to the device's manufacturer. If it detects a signal from a nearby hardware device made by Meta or Snap, the app will send an alert to the user.

This development is significant because it highlights concerns about surveillance and harassment using smart glasses. TechCrunch notes that companies like Meta are planning to add facial recognition features to their products, which could lead to increased privacy invasions. The Nearby Glasses app aims to help users protect themselves from these potential threats by providing an early warning system.

The article also touches on the issue of data sovereignty and power consolidation, as companies like Meta continue to collect and use personal data without explicit consent. However, these themes are secondary to the app's primary function of detecting wearable surveillance devices.

### [New Mexico Dems Pass An Affordable Broadband Law In 25 Days](https://www.techdirt.com/2026/03/02/new-mexico-dems-pass-an-affordable-broadband-law-in-25-days/)
*Tech Dirt*

The New Mexico state legislature has passed a law providing affordable broadband access to low-income residents, with the Low-Income Telecommunications Assistance Program (LITAP) offering up to $30 per month in assistance. This program is funded by the state universal service fund and aims to address the lack of affordable broadband access in New Mexico.

The new law was drafted and passed in just 25 days, a swift process that highlights the urgency of addressing internet affordability issues. The program's creation comes after the federal Affordable Connectivity Program (ACP) was killed by Trump Republicans in 2024, despite generating significant downstream benefits for low-income households.

A study found that the ACP generated between $28.9 and $29.5 billion in savings annually due to expanded access to affordable internet, remote work opportunities, online education tools, and remote telehealth services. The program also increased annual earnings for students by $3.7 billion and generated $2.1 to $4.3 billion in annual wage gains from expanded labor force participation.

### [OpenAI’s ‘Red Lines’ Are Written In The NSA’s Dictionary—Where Words Mean What The NSA Wants Them To Mean](https://www.techdirt.com/2026/03/02/openais-red-lines-are-written-in-the-nsas-dictionary-where-words-mean-what-the-nsa-wants-them-to-mean/)
*Tech Dirt*

The article reports that OpenAI's contract with the Department of War includes "red lines" that supposedly prohibit the use of its technology for mass domestic surveillance, autonomous weapons systems, and high-stakes automated decisions. However, Tech Dirt found that these "red lines" are actually defined by the NSA's dictionary, which redefines common English words to permit surveillance and other activities.

According to the article, OpenAI's contract cites Executive Order 12333 as a legal authority for its actions, but this order is actually a loophole that allows the intelligence community to surveil Americans' communications. By referencing this order, OpenAI has effectively adopted the NSA's dictionary, which means that its "red lines" are not as strict as they seem.

Tech Dirt reports that this is not an isolated incident, but rather part of a long-standing practice by the NSA to redefine words and phrases to justify its actions. This has been exposed through whistleblower testimony, declassified documents, and court cases. The article cites specific examples from OpenAI's contract language, which uses phrases like "defined foreign intelligence purpose" to justify surveillance.
