### [Hacktivist scrapes over 500,000 stalkerware customers’ payment records](https://techcrunch.com/2026/02/09/hacktivist-scrapes-over-500000-stalkerware-customers-payment-records/)
*TechCrunch*

A hacktivist has scraped over 500,000 payment records from a Ukrainian company called Struktura, which provides consumer-grade stalkerware phone surveillance apps. The data includes email addresses and partial payment information of customers who paid to spy on others using services like Geofinder, uMobix, and Peekviewer.

This incident is significant because it exposes the vulnerability of surveillance vendors' cybersecurity and highlights concerns about the use of stalkerware apps. TechCrunch reports that this is not an isolated incident, with dozens of stalkerware apps having been hacked or losing users' private data in recent years due to poor security practices by their operators.

The hacktivist claimed to have found a "trivial" bug in Struktura's website that allowed them to access the data. The scraped data was published on a hacking forum, further emphasizing the risks associated with using stalkerware apps and the potential consequences of their operators' security flaws.

TechCrunch notes that this is just one example of a surveillance vendor exposing customer information due to security vulnerabilities.

### [Op-ed: Weakening Section 230 Would Chill Online Speech](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2026/02/op-ed-weakening-section-230-would-chill-online-speech)
*EFF*

Section 230, a law enacted 30 years ago, provides immunity to online services from most civil suits based on user-generated content. EFF reports that weakening this provision would lead to massive depublication of online content and stifle real-time user-generated speech.

The op-ed argues that Section 230 is essential for preserving free expression online, allowing millions of people to publish their thoughts, photos, and jokes without fear of liability. The author claims that alternatives to immunity would result in censorship and a chilling effect on online speech.

> "Section 230, “the 26 words that created the internet,” was enacted 30 years ago this week. It was no rush-job—rather, it was the result of wise legislative deliberation and foresight, and it remains the best bulwark to protect free expression online." — [Op-ed: Weakening Section 230 Would Chill Online Speech](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2026/02/op-ed-weakening-section-230-would-chill-online-speech)
— [EFF](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2026/02/op-ed-weakening-section-230-would-chill-online-speech)


The EFF op-ed emphasizes that Congress' purposeful choice of immunity in Section 230 has allowed the internet to thrive as a platform for free expression, connecting people worldwide and enabling them to share ideas without needing immense resources or technical expertise.

### [Chatbots Make Terrible Doctors, New Study Finds](https://www.404media.co/chatbots-health-medical-advice-study/)
*404 Media*

A new study published in Nature Medicine found that large language models (LLMs) are not reliable medical advisors. Researchers tested 1,298 UK-based participants who were given a choice of LLMs or allowed to use a source they preferred to make decisions about medical scenarios. When the LLMs provided advice directly, they correctly identified conditions in most cases. However, when interacting with users, the LLMs often generated incorrect or incomplete information.

This study highlights the limitations of AI systems in healthcare, specifically large language models, which can provide misleading or inadequate guidance to patients. The findings suggest that patients should be aware of the risks of relying on chatbots for medical advice and consider seeking human care instead.

### [Discord to roll out age verification next month for full access to its platform](https://techcrunch.com/2026/02/09/discord-to-roll-out-age-verification-next-month-for-full-access-to-its-platform/)
*TechCrunch*

Discord is introducing global age verification starting next month, requiring users to prove they are adults to access certain settings and age-restricted content. Users will be defaulted into a "teen-appropriate experience" unless they complete age verification, which can be done through facial age estimation or submitting an ID to Discord's vendor partners.

This move follows similar efforts by other online platforms, such as Roblox and YouTube, to strengthen child safety. However, the implementation of age verification has raised concerns about data protection and potential biases in facial age estimation solutions. TechCrunch reports that user data will be deleted quickly after age confirmation, but a previous security incident highlighted vulnerabilities in its third-party vendor relationships.

Discord users will need to be confirmed as adults to unblur sensitive content or turn off certain settings, access age-restricted channels, servers, and app commands, and modify other settings. To complete age verification, users can either submit a video selfie for facial age estimation or provide an ID to Discord's vendor partners. The platform plans to add more options in the future.

This development matters because it highlights the trade-offs between safety measures and individual freedoms. As TechCrunch notes, Discord is rolling out age verification globally starting next month, with users being defaulted into a "teen-appropriate experience" unless they complete age verification.

### [Silicon Valley can’t import talent like before. So it’s exporting jobs](https://restofworld.org/2026/h1b-visa-impact-india-tech-hiring-faamng/?utm_source=rss&utm_medium=rss&utm_campaign=feeds)
*Rest of World*

US tech companies are facing a challenge attracting top talent due to strict immigration policies and visa restrictions. As a result, they're shifting their recruitment strategies by hiring in countries with more favorable labor markets, such as India. Major firms like Meta, Amazon, Apple, Microsoft, Netflix, and Google have increased their hiring in India.

This development is driven by the difficulty of obtaining H-1B visas for foreign workers, making it harder for US companies to access global talent. The trend may be a long-term consequence of the Trump administration's policies on immigration and visa restrictions.
