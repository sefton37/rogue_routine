### [Copyright Kills Competition](https://www.techdirt.com/2026/02/13/copyright-kills-competition/)
*Tech Dirt*

Today's data reveals that powerful companies are using copyright laws to maintain their dominance in the market, stifling competition and innovation. According to Tech Dirt, this has resulted in a concentration of power among a few corporate gatekeepers, harming creators and consumers alike.

Tech Dirt reports that requiring developers to license AI training materials would benefit large tech companies at the expense of smaller competitors, limiting access to information and tools for emerging creators. This is evident in cases such as Thomson Reuters v. Ross Intelligence, where a startup was forced out of business due to a copyright lawsuit over using works to train an AI tool.

> "Copyright owners increasingly claim more draconian copyright law and policy will fight back against big tech companies. In reality, copyright gives the most powerful companies even more control over creators and competitors." [Tech Dirt](https://www.techdirt.com/2026/02/13/copyright-kills-competition/)

Per Tech Dirt, proponents of stronger copyright laws claim they will help creators, but experts argue that this would only serve to further entrench the power of large corporations. The article highlights how legacy gatekeepers have used copyright to stifle access to information and create new tools, ultimately benefiting themselves at the expense of emerging creators.

This trend is part of a broader pattern where powerful companies use regulatory frameworks to maintain their dominance in the market, stifling competition and innovation. Today's data shows that this has resulted in a concentration of power among a few corporate gatekeepers, harming creators and consumers alike.

### [Seven Billion Reasons for Facebook to Abandon its Face Recognition Plans](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2026/02/seven-billion-reasons-facebook-abandon-its-face-recognition-plans)
*EFF*

Today's data reveals that Meta is considering adding face recognition technology to its smart glasses, despite a history of controversy and significant risks associated with this feature.

The article, published by EFF, reports that Meta has faced numerous legal battles and settlements related to its use of face recognition, including a $7 billion settlement in 2021. Despite this, the company is moving forward with plans to integrate face recognition into its smart glasses, citing a "dynamic political environment" where civil society groups may be less likely to oppose it.

This development highlights the ongoing tension between corporate interests and user privacy. EFF argues that Meta should abandon its plans due to the potential harm caused by mass surveillance, data breach, and discrimination associated with biometric data collection. The article emphasizes that users cannot provide informed consent for face recognition technology, as they would need to opt-in every time they step into view of the camera-equipped glasses.

As EFF details, dozens of state laws consider biometric information to be sensitive and require companies to implement strict protections to collect and process it, including affirmative consent. Meta's decision to move forward with face recognition despite these risks raises questions about its commitment to user privacy.

The article also notes that Meta has a history of abandoning similar technology after facing backlash from users and regulators. In 2021, the company shut down its tool that scanned the face of every person in photos posted on the platform and deleted over a billion face templates.

Overall, today's data shows that corporate interests continue to prioritize profits over user privacy, despite growing concerns about the risks associated with biometric data collection. As EFF argues, Meta should abandon its plans for face recognition technology to avoid further harm to users and potential legal repercussions.

### [Cops Criticize Flock Safety After It’s Caught Handing Out Access To Federal Agencies](https://www.techdirt.com/2026/02/13/cops-criticize-flock-safety-after-its-caught-handing-out-access-to-federal-agencies/)
*Tech Dirt*

Today's data highlights a concerning trend of unchecked access to sensitive information, with federal agencies exploiting Flock Safety's nationwide search setting to access license plate reader records without permission.

According to Tech Dirt, at least six offices from four federal agencies accessed the Mountain View police department's Flock Safety system between August and November 2024, raising questions about data sovereignty and attention economy. This incident is not an isolated case; Flock Safety has previously been linked to surveillance of abortion seekers and protesters.

Tech Dirt reports that Flock Safety's default nationwide search setting allowed federal agencies to access hundreds of millions of license plate records without explicit permission from the police department. This lack of control over user data and systemic design flaws have significant implications for individual privacy and power consolidation in the attention economy.

As Tech Dirt details, this incident highlights the need for stricter regulations on data access and usage, particularly when it comes to sensitive information like license plate reader records. The unchecked exploitation of Flock Safety's system by federal agencies raises concerns about the potential for widespread surveillance and erosion of civil liberties.

### [UK Supreme Court Affirms Ruling That Oatly Can’t Use ‘Milk’ In Its Almond Milk Branding](https://www.techdirt.com/2026/02/13/uk-supreme-court-affirms-ruling-that-oatly-cant-use-milk-in-its-almond-milk-branding/)
*Tech Dirt*

Today's data reveals a significant development in the UK's trademark dispute landscape: the UK Supreme Court has upheld a ruling that Oatly cannot use the slogan "Post Milk Generation" on its oat-based products due to a trademark dispute with Dairy UK.

This decision is notable because it appears to favor the dairy industry over foreign competition, potentially limiting consumer choice. The implications of this ruling are significant for companies like Oatly, which rely on distinctive branding to differentiate themselves in a crowded market. As Tech Dirt notes, the decision is seen as protectionist, allowing Dairy UK to limit competition from foreign companies. This development highlights the ongoing tension between consumer choice and industry interests in the UK's trademark landscape.

The article excerpt provides further context: "Oatly stated that the reversing of the decision was 'absurd' and would have a chilling effect on innovation." This quote underscores the potential consequences of this ruling for businesses operating in the UK.

### [Fintech lending giant Figure confirms data breach](https://techcrunch.com/2026/02/13/fintech-lending-giant-figure-confirms-data-breach/)
*TechCrunch*

Figure Technology, a blockchain-based lending company, has confirmed a data breach after hackers stole sensitive customer information through a social engineering attack.

The breach is significant because it highlights the vulnerability of fintech companies to cyber attacks, with TechCrunch reporting that Figure's spokesperson stated the breach originated when an employee was tricked into sharing files with the attackers. This suggests that even companies with robust security measures in place can still be compromised by human error.

Per TechCrunch, the hacking group ShinyHunters took responsibility for the hack and published 2.5 gigabytes of allegedly stolen data online, including customers' full names, home addresses, dates of birth, and phone numbers. This incident is part of a larger trend of data breaches targeting fintech companies and online platforms.

The breach also raises questions about the security of single sign-on providers like Okta, which was used by Figure's employees to access their accounts. TechCrunch reports that ShinyHunters claimed to have targeted customers who rely on Okta, including Harvard University and the University of Pennsylvania.
