### [Copyright Kills Competition](https://www.techdirt.com/2026/02/13/copyright-kills-competition/)
*Tech Dirt*

Here's a detailed analysis of the article:

The article [Copyright Kills Competition](https://www.techdirt.com/2026/02/13/copyright-kills-competition/) by Tech Dirt reveals how powerful companies are using copyright laws to maintain their dominance in the market, stifling competition and innovation. According to the article, this concentration of power among corporate gatekeepers is harming creators and consumers alike. The use of copyright law to limit access to information and training data for AI development is particularly problematic, as it would benefit large tech companies at the expense of smaller competitors.

The article highlights how legacy gatekeepers have used copyright to stifle access to information and create new tools, while also noting that pro-monopoly regulation through copyright won't provide meaningful economic support for vulnerable artists and creators. This is evident in cases such as Thomson Reuters v. Ross Intelligence, where a startup was forced out of business due to a copyright lawsuit over using works to train an AI tool.

The article emphasizes the need for a system that supports grassroots innovation and emerging creators by lowering barriers to entry, ultimately offering all of us a wider variety of choices. This is in contrast to pro-monopoly regulation through copyright, which would only serve to further entrench the power of large corporations.

This is a critical issue, as it affects not only the tech industry but also the broader economy and society.

The article's emphasis on the concentration of power among corporate gatekeepers and the harm caused to creators and consumers alike is particularly noteworthy. It also highlights the need for a more nuanced understanding of how copyright laws are being used in the digital age, and how they can be reformed to promote innovation and competition rather than stifling it.

### [Seven Billion Reasons for Facebook to Abandon its Face Recognition Plans](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2026/02/seven-billion-reasons-facebook-abandon-its-face-recognition-plans)
*EFF*

According to EFF, Meta is considering adding face recognition technology to its smart glasses, despite a history of facing legal battles and settlements related to this feature. This move has sparked criticism from experts who argue that it would be a bad idea due to concerns about mass surveillance, data breach, and discrimination.

The article reveals the power dynamics at play when a tech giant like Meta pushes forward with potentially invasive features, disregarding user privacy and potential harm. By highlighting the risks associated with face recognition technology, EFF emphasizes the importance of prioritizing user rights over profits. The fact that Meta is considering launching this feature despite past legal battles and settlements suggests a disregard for accountability.

The article also highlights the systemic problems surrounding biometric data collection and its implications on individual privacy. EFF warns that collecting and processing biometric information without consent would be a violation of users' rights, particularly in light of dozens of state laws that consider biometric information to be sensitive and require strict protections.

As EFF notes, "Meta should already know the privacy risks of face recognition technology" after abandoning related technology and paying nearly $7 billion in settlements a few years ago. This suggests that Meta is aware of the potential consequences but is choosing to ignore them for financial gain.

The story matters because it reveals the tension between corporate interests and user rights, as well as the lack of accountability in the tech industry. By emphasizing the importance of prioritizing user privacy over profits, EFF highlights the need for greater transparency and regulation in the tech sector.

This incident serves as a reminder that even with advanced security measures, companies can still fall victim to sophisticated cyber attacks. TechCrunch's report on this breach is a timely warning about the risks associated with online transactions and the need for greater vigilance in protecting personal data.
