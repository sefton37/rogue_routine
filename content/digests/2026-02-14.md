## The Big Picture
Today's top stories reveal a disturbing trend where powerful companies are using copyright laws to maintain their dominance in the market and stifle competition. According to Tech Dirt, [Copyright Kills Competition](https://www.techdirt.com/2026/02/13/copyright-kills-competition/) highlights how legacy gatekeepers have used copyright law to limit access to information and training data for AI development, benefiting large tech companies at the expense of smaller competitors. Meanwhile, Meta's decision to add face recognition technology to its smart glasses, despite controversy and significant risks, raises concerns about user rights and privacy.

In a related story, news publishers blocking access to the Internet Archive due to fears of AI scraping highlights the tension between data sovereignty and the preservation of historical records. This move has far-reaching consequences for researchers', historians', and journalists' ability to study and learn from the past. Furthermore, Flock Safety's automated license plate reader system being exploited by federal agencies without permission underscores concerns about power consolidation in the attention economy.

These stories collectively reveal a systemic problem where powerful companies are using various means – copyright laws, surveillance technologies, and data control – to maintain their dominance and limit competition, ultimately harming creators and consumers. They highlight the need for a more nuanced discussion about data sovereignty, AI scraping, and the preservation of historical records.

## Deep Dives

### [Copyright Kills Competition](https://www.techdirt.com/2026/02/13/copyright-kills-competition/)
*Tech Dirt*

According to Tech Dirt, the article [Copyright Kills Competition](https://www.techdirt.com/2026/02/13/copyright-kills-competition/) reveals a disturbing trend where powerful companies are using copyright laws to maintain their dominance in the market and stifle competition. This has resulted in a concentration of power among a few corporate gatekeepers, harming creators and consumers alike.

The article highlights how legacy gatekeepers have used copyright law to limit access to information and training data for AI development, benefiting large tech companies at the expense of smaller competitors. For example, it mentions the case of Thomson Reuters v. Ross Intelligence, where a startup was forced out of business due to a copyright lawsuit over using works to train an AI tool.

> "Copyright owners increasingly claim more draconian copyright law and policy will fight back against big tech companies. In reality, copyright gives the most powerful companies even more control over creators and competitors."
— [Tech Dirt](https://www.techdirt.com/2026/02/13/copyright-kills-competition/)


Tech Dirt reports that proponents of stronger copyright laws claim they will help creators, but experts argue that this would only serve to further entrench the power of large corporations. The article emphasizes that pro-monopoly regulation through copyright won't provide any meaningful economic support for vulnerable artists and creators.

The story matters because it reveals a systemic problem where powerful companies are using copyright law to maintain their dominance and limit competition, ultimately harming creators and consumers. It highlights the need for a system that supports grassroots innovation and emerging creators by lowering barriers to entry.

This story matters because it reveals the tension between corporate interests and user rights. Meta's decision-making process prioritizes profits over user privacy, which is concerning given the company's history of facing legal battles and settlements related to its use of face recognition technology. The article emphasizes that biometric data, such as faceprints, are highly sensitive and require strict protections.

The article also reveals a power imbalance between tech companies like Meta and their users. By pushing forward with face recognition plans despite known risks, Meta is exercising control over users' personal information without their consent. This raises questions about the accountability of tech giants and their responsibility to protect user data.

As EFF reports, "Your biometric data... are some of the most sensitive pieces of data that a company can collect." The article argues that Meta should abandon its plans due to the potential harm it could cause, rather than relying on forced compliance. This highlights the importance of prioritizing user rights and privacy over corporate interests.

The story also touches on the theme of systemic design and incentive architecture. By prioritizing profits over user safety, Meta's decision-making process is flawed and may lead to further controversy and legal battles. The article suggests that companies like Meta should be held accountable for their actions and prioritize transparency and user consent in their product development processes.

Overall, this story highlights the need for greater accountability and transparency from tech giants like Meta, as well as a renewed focus on protecting user rights and privacy in the face of emerging technologies.

### [News Publishers Are Now Blocking The Internet Archive, And We May All Regret It](https://www.techdirt.com/2026/02/13/news-publishers-are-now-blocking-the-internet-archive-and-we-may-all-regret-it/)
*Tech Dirt*

**Daily Briefing:**

This article reveals a concerning trend where major news publishers are blocking access to the Internet Archive due to concerns about AI scraping. According to Tech Dirt, The Guardian and The New York Times have limited what the Internet Archive can crawl and preserve, effectively restricting researchers', historians', and journalists' ability to access quality journalism.

The move is noteworthy because it highlights the tension between data sovereignty and the preservation of historical records. By blocking access to the archive, news publishers risk creating a biased historical record that systematically excludes quality journalism. This decision has significant implications for the democratization of tools and access, as it may limit researchers' ability to study and learn from the past.

The article reveals how the fear of AI is leading news publishers to prioritize their own interests over the public's right to access information. This decision has far-reaching consequences, as it undermines the principle of open access and limits our ability to learn from history.

This story matters because it shows how power dynamics are playing out in the attention economy. By restricting access to the Internet Archive, news publishers are exercising control over what is preserved and what is lost. This move highlights the need for a more nuanced discussion about data sovereignty, AI scraping, and the preservation of historical records.

Flock Safety's handling of user data has been criticized before, particularly in cases involving surveillance of abortion seekers and protesters. This latest incident only adds to the growing concerns about the company's role in perpetuating a culture of mass surveillance.
