## The Big Picture
What is the true nature of the relationship between tech companies and governments when it comes to regulating AI use, particularly in areas like surveillance and military applications? This complex web of power dynamics involves a delicate balance between tech companies' claims of concern about AI misuse and their influence on shaping policy and public discourse.

Today's articles reveal that Anthropic's stance on Claude is closely tied to its defense industry clients' concerns about potential misuse. For instance, Anthropic's refusal to grant unrestricted access to its AI model Claude to the US military raises questions about the company's commitment to preventing AI misuse (as reported in [The Anthropic-DOD Conflict: Privacy Protections Shouldn’t Depend On the Decisions of a Few Powerful People](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2026/03/anthropic-dod-conflict-privacy-protections-shouldnt-depend-decisions-few-powerful)). Meanwhile, Google's Gemini chatbot being used to drive a man into fatal delusion highlights the need for stronger regulation and accountability measures (as reported in [Father sues Google, claiming Gemini chatbot drove son into fatal delusion](https://techcrunch.com/2026/03/04/father-sues-google-claiming-gemini-chatbot-drove-son-into-fatal-delusion/)).

## Deep Dives

### [The Anthropic-DOD Conflict: Privacy Protections Shouldn’t Depend On the Decisions of a Few Powerful People](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2026/03/anthropic-dod-conflict-privacy-protections-shouldnt-depend-decisions-few-powerful)
*EFF*

What happens when a tech company refuses to grant unrestricted access to its AI technology to the US military, citing concerns about potential misuse? The article reports that Anthropic, an AI company, has ended its $200 million contract with the Pentagon due to this dispute.

The conflict highlights the tension between tech companies' efforts to protect users' privacy and the government's desire to use AI for surveillance and military purposes. As EFF notes, this issue is not isolated, but rather part of a broader pattern where the state of one's privacy is being decided by contract negotiations between giant tech companies and the US government.

The article emphasizes that relying on CEOs to do the right thing is not a sustainable solution for protecting users' rights. Instead, it calls for proactive legal restrictions to prevent the government from using AI for mass surveillance and autonomous weapons systems.

Anthropic's CEO, Dario Amodei, has stated in an interview that Congress should take responsibility for regulating AI use, rather than relying on private sector companies to make decisions about user privacy.

The article raises questions about the role of tech companies in protecting users' rights and the need for stronger legal protections against government overreach.

### [Rubio To World: Stop Doing The Exact Same Thing The US Just Did](https://www.techdirt.com/2026/03/03/rubio-to-world-stop-doing-the-exact-same-thing-the-us-just-did/)
*Tech Dirt*

The US State Department has issued a cable ordering diplomats to lobby against data sovereignty and localization initiatives around the world, citing concerns about disrupting global data flows and increasing cybersecurity risks. However, this move comes after the US government itself implemented similar policies, such as forcing TikTok to divest its US operations due to national security concerns.

This article highlights the hypocrisy of the US administration in pushing for open data flows while engaging in protectionist trade policies and undermining international cooperation. The State Department's cable argues that data localization mandates are bad for the internet, but this stance is undermined by the US government's own actions on TikTok. Experts argue that the US has eroded trust among allies, who are now rushing to build digital walls in response.

The article notes that while the policy position described in the cable is correct, the messenger has lost credibility due to past actions. The justification for the US government's data localization policies kept changing depending on the day and who you talked to, making it difficult to take their current stance seriously.

Tech Dirt reports that the Trump administration pushed for a more assertive international data policy, which included forcing TikTok to divest its US operations. This move was seen as a thinly veiled mechanism for government surveillance and control of information, raising concerns about the erosion of civil liberties.

The article also highlights the impact of the US government's actions on global cooperation. The cable ordering diplomats to lobby against data sovereignty and localization initiatives is seen as an attempt to undermine international efforts to establish digital sovereignty. This move has been met with skepticism by allies who are now rushing to build their own digital walls in response.

### [AI companies are spending millions to thwart this former tech exec’s congressional bid](https://techcrunch.com/2026/03/03/ai-companies-are-spending-millions-to-thwart-this-former-tech-execs-congressional-bid/)
*TechCrunch*

What's clear from this article is that AI companies are aggressively using their financial resources to influence US politics and prevent regulation of their industry.

The super PAC, Leading the Future, backed by Palantir co-founder Joe Lonsdale and OpenAI President Greg Brockman, has spent millions on ads attacking Alex Bores, a former tech executive running for Congress. This is not just about personal attacks; it's about silencing a candidate who sponsors legislation that regulates AI safety and disclosure.

The article highlights the significant influence of pro-AI PACs in US politics, particularly in state elections where AI legislation is being introduced. TechCrunch reports that Leading the Future has raised $125 million to go after candidates like Bores and support those with a light-to-no-touch approach to regulating AI.

Bores claims that his background working in tech makes him a credible candidate for federal AI regulation, but Leading the Future's goal is not to have a conversation about AI regulation, according to TechCrunch. Instead, they aim to intimidate elected officials who disagree with their views.

The article raises questions about the extent of Big Tech's influence on US politics and whether this kind of spending will continue unabated.
