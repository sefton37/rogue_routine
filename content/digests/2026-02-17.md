## The Big Picture
Today's stories reveal a stark contrast between governments' glowing reviews of AI-powered initiatives and their actual practices, which often prioritize profit over transparency and accountability. The simultaneous FTC investigation into Apple and the European Parliament's ban on AI tools for lawmakers highlight a disconnect between Western governments' rhetoric on AI regulation and their actual willingness to enforce accountability.

### ['Students Are Being Treated Like Guinea Pigs:' Inside an AI-Powered Private School](https://www.404media.co/students-are-being-treated-like-guinea-pigs-inside-an-ai-powered-private-school/)
*404 Media*

The article reveals that Alpha School, a private institution, is using AI to teach students and has been praised by media outlets such as Fox News and The New York Times. However, internal company documents and former employees reveal that the school's reliance on generative AI is producing faulty lesson plans, collecting sensitive student data without permission, and making students anxious due to constant monitoring.

This development highlights a concerning trend in education technology: the prioritization of profit over student well-being. Alpha School promises to deliver high-quality education in just two hours a day, but critics argue that this approach is flawed and focuses on generating revenue rather than providing effective learning experiences for students.

The article also exposes the collection of sensitive student data without permission, which is stored in a Google Drive folder accessible by anyone with the link. This raises questions about data sovereignty and who has control over student information.

As 404 Media reports, former employees have expressed concerns about the quality of AI-generated lessons, citing unclear wording and illogical choices in multiple-choice questions that "fail to meet SAT standards." These findings suggest that Alpha School's business model is flawed, prioritizing profit over student success.

The article does not reveal any information about the long-term consequences of using AI-powered education or how students are performing academically. It also does not explore alternative approaches to education technology that prioritize student well-being and effective learning experiences.

Overall, this article highlights the need for critical examination of education technology and its impact on students. By prioritizing profit over student success, Alpha School is treating students like guinea pigs in a larger experiment to test the limits of AI-powered education.

### [Trump FTC Threatens Apple With A Fake Investigation Into Its Nonexistent ‘Liberal News Bias’](https://www.techdirt.com/2026/02/17/trump-ftc-threatens-apple-with-a-fake-investigation-into-its-nonexistent-liberal-news-bias/)
*Tech Dirt*

The Trump Federal Trade Commission (FTC) has launched a fake investigation into Apple's news feed, claiming it promotes liberal outlets and suppresses conservative ones. Tech Dirt reports that this move is a partisan attempt to intimidate companies into catering to Trump supporters' ideologies.

This development highlights the FTC's willingness to use its power to silence dissenting voices and promote right-wing propaganda. The alleged bias in Apple News is based on a flawed study by the Media Research Center, which was cited in an article by Rupert Murdoch's New York Post. Tech Dirt notes that this "study" looked at a small sample size of 620 articles promoted by Apple News and arbitrarily declared 440 of them as having a "liberal bias."

The FTC's action is a clear threat to free expression and the First Amendment in the US. Even if it were true that Apple was biased in its news feed, that would still be within its rights under the First Amendment. The fact that the FTC is trying to silence Apple for allegedly promoting liberal outlets shows how far they're willing to go to appease Trump supporters.

The article also reveals how easily the FTC can use its power to silence companies and intimidate them into bending to their will. This move sets a dangerous precedent, where any company can be targeted by the FTC for perceived ideological bias. As Tech Dirt notes, this is a clear violation of the First Amendment and an attack on free expression.

The Trump FTC's fake investigation into Apple News also highlights the blurred lines between corporate media bias and partisan politics. The article mentions how some outlets, like the Washington Post, have become more right-wing under Jeff Bezos' ownership, but are still labeled as "liberal" by the Media Research Center.

As for what this article does not reveal, it focuses primarily on the FTC's action against Apple News without delving deeper into the broader implications of corporate media bias and partisan politics. The article also relies heavily on Tech Dirt's reporting, which may have its own biases and perspectives. Additionally, the article does not explore the potential consequences of the FTC's actions on other companies or industries.

The report aims to help journalists uncover the truth behind the sales hype and provide a framework for asking better questions about the costs, benefits, and accountability of police surveillance technology. ### [European Parliament blocks AI on lawmakers’ devices, citing security risks](https://techcrunch.com/2026/02/17/european-parliament-blocks-ai-on-lawmakers-devices-citing-security-risks/)
*TechCrunch*

The European Parliament has blocked lawmakers from using AI tools on their work devices due to concerns over cybersecurity and privacy risks. Specifically, the parliament's IT department cited uncertainty about the security of data uploaded to AI companies' servers and the potential for sensitive information to be shared among users.

This decision is significant because it highlights the EU's strict data protection rules and its willingness to take action against US tech giants that may compromise user data. The move also underscores the power consolidation of these companies, which are subject to unpredictable demands from the Trump administration through subpoenas.

TechCrunch reports that the parliament's IT department disabled AI features on lawmakers' devices due to concerns about uploading confidential correspondence to the cloud. This decision was made despite the European Commission's recent proposals to relax data protection rules and make it easier for US tech giants to operate in the EU.

The article does not delve into the specifics of how these subpoenas work or what exactly is being demanded from tech companies. It also does not explore the potential consequences of relaxing data protection rules, which could have significant implications for user privacy and security.

The article reveals how gig platforms manipulate human attention and labor for profit, highlighting the need for new economic models that prioritize worker well-being over corporate interests. However, it does not explore the broader implications of this manipulation on society or the potential consequences of a jobs guarantee on the economy as a whole.

### [Australia’s Social Media Ban Is Isolating Kids With Disabilities—Just Like Critics Warned](https://www.techdirt.com/2026/02/17/australias-social-media-ban-is-isolating-kids-with-disabilities-just-like-critics-warned/)
*Tech Dirt*

The article reports that Australia's social media ban for kids under 16 has had a disproportionate impact on children with disabilities, who relied on online platforms to connect with friends and communities. According to Tech Dirt, this ban was intended to protect young people from potential harms of social media, but critics argue it has isolated those most vulnerable.

Tech Dirt notes that real-world stories are emerging of kids being harmed by the law, which is a stark contrast to theory and data suggesting that social media is not inherently harmful. The article highlights the case of Indy, a 14-year-old autistic girl who felt lonely and disconnected after the ban took effect because she had used social media as a safe space to connect with friends.

The article also mentions how children's advocacy groups have warned that the ban is isolating kids with disabilities, who are already isolated from their peers. This is significant because it shows how a well-intentioned policy can have unintended consequences and exacerbate existing systemic issues.

### [Just 8 months in, India’s vibe-coding startup Emergent claims ARR of over $100M](https://techcrunch.com/2026/02/17/emergent-hits-100m-arr-eight-months-after-launch-rolls-out-mobile-app/)
*TechCrunch*

Emergent, an Indian vibe-coding startup, has reached an annual run-rate revenue of over $100 million just eight months after its launch, with more than 6 million users worldwide and 150,000 paying customers.

This rapid growth is driven by the increasing demand for vibe-coding solutions, which enable non-technical users to build production-ready applications quickly and easily. TechCrunch reports that nearly 40% of Emergent's users are small businesses, and about 70% have no prior coding experience. These users mostly use the platform to digitize operations previously run on spreadsheets, email or messaging apps, and to build custom software.

The startup competes with other vibe-coding platforms like Replit, Lovable, Rocket.new, and Wabi, but its focus on small businesses and mobile app development has contributed to its success. TechCrunch found that Emergent's users have created over 7 million applications on the platform.

### [As AI jitters rattle IT stocks, Infosys partners with Anthropic to build ‘enterprise-grade’ AI agents](https://techcrunch.com/2026/02/17/as-ai-jitters-rattle-it-stocks-infosys-partners-with-anthropic-to-build-enterprise-grade-ai-agents/)

Infosys, a leading Indian IT services company, is partnering with Anthropic to develop "enterprise-grade" AI agents that can autonomously handle complex workflows across various industries. This move comes as concerns about AI disruption in India's $280 billion IT services industry grow, where labor-intensive outsourcing business models are being challenged by automation driven by large language models.

The partnership aims to integrate Anthropic's Claude models into Infosys' Topaz AI platform, enabling the creation of agentic systems that can work in regulated environments. This strategic step is seen as a way for Anthropic to expand into heavily regulated enterprise sectors, while Infosys seeks to leverage its expertise in industries such as financial services and manufacturing.

### [Mistral AI buys Koyeb in first acquisition to back its cloud ambitions](https://techcrunch.com/2026/02/17/mistral-ai-buys-koyeb-in-first-acquisition-to-back-its-cloud-ambitions/)
*TechCrunch*

Mistral AI, a French company valued at $13.8 billion, has made its first acquisition by buying Koyeb, a Paris-based startup that simplifies AI app deployment and manages infrastructure. This deal confirms Mistral's ambitions to become a full-stack player in the AI industry, expanding beyond large language models (LLMs) to cloud infrastructure.

The acquisition will help Mistral deploy models directly on clients' hardware, optimize GPU use, and scale AI inference. Koyeb's technology will also enable Mistral to provide isolated environments for deploying AI agents, as seen in Koyeb Sandboxes. This move is significant because it marks a shift in Mistral's focus from developing LLMs to building cloud infrastructure.

Mistral's acquisition of Koyeb is part of its efforts to build sovereign AI infrastructure in Europe. By expanding its capabilities and presence in the region, Mistral aims to become a major player in the global AI market. TechCrunch reports that Koyeb's team will join Mistral's engineering team, further solidifying the company's position in the industry.
