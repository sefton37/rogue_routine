**Key fact:** Section 1201 of the Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA) is being used to restrict access to copyrighted works, effectively turning any conduct that displeases a business into a felony.

**Significance:** This law makes it a crime to bypass an "effective access control" for a copyrighted work, which can be as simple as altering software on a device. The penalties for getting crosswise with DMCA 1201 are severe, and few people have tried to litigate its contours. A case from 2000 involving a service called Aimster, which was shut down by the record industry, highlights how unclear the guidelines for "effective" access control remain.

**Implication:** This law concentrates power in the hands of businesses, allowing them to dictate what constitutes an "effective" access control and potentially restricting digital access. The lack of clear guidelines for this law raises concerns about data sovereignty and systemic design.

### [Former Federal Judge: ICE’s Home Raiding Policy Violates A Basic Constitutional Right](https://www.techdirt.com/2026/02/06/former-federal-judge-ices-home-raiding-policy-violates-a-basic-constitutional-right/)
*Tech Dirt*

A former federal judge, John E. Jones III, has spoken out against Immigration and Customs Enforcement's (ICE) policy of raiding homes without a judicial warrant. The policy, outlined in an internal memo, asserts that immigration officers can enter a home without a judge's warrant, which is seen as a reversal of longstanding guidance meant to respect constitutional limits on government searches.

The Fourth Amendment protects individuals from unreasonable searches and seizures, requiring a warrant issued by a judicial officer based on probable cause. The former judge argues that ICE's policy turns this amendment "on its head" and amounts to an abuse of power. This development is significant because it highlights the potential for unchecked authority in immigration enforcement, potentially infringing on citizens' rights.

ICE agents have been using aggressive methods to make arrests, including breaking down doors in Minneapolis homes. The internal memo was acquired via a whistleblower and has sparked concerns about the erosion of constitutional protections.
